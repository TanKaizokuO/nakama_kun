Abstract—Most deepfake detection methods use supervised
visual classifiers developed from curated datasets. While these
methods work well in a controlled setting, they have demon-
strated limited generalization capabilities with respect to distribu-
tional changes, recompression, and adversarial post-processing.
In addition, the black-box nature of these classifiers makes
it difficult for practitioners to get much interpretability from
them in any forensic or evidentiary-related scenarios. This
paper proposes an explainable multimodal deepfake detection
framework based on the integration of multiple forms of evidence
including metadata/encoding trace consistency, physiological sig-
nal verification, and visual artifacts. Specifically, the metadata
consistency module identifies structural inconsistencies in the
encoding (e.g., any variation from what would typically exist in
an authentic instance based on timestamp, codec structure, and
provenance); the physiological verification module verifies the
biological plausibility (e.g., through estimating peak and trough
of pulse measurements using remote photoplethysmography);
and the visual artifact localization module uses convolutional
neural networks (CNN) with Grad-CAM assignment to identify
and/or localize visual artifacts relating to the manipulation. All
forms of evidence from each module are integrated together by
using a cross-evidence reasoning module, which explicitly models
the agreement/disagreement between all sources of evidence to
facilitate robustly and interpretably making a determination.
This framework has created a standardized way to create
forensic reports by including anomaly scores, visual evidence
specific to the location of the anomaly, and explanations of
how the detected anomaly was created (modality specific). The
experimental evaluation of the FaceForensics++, DFDC, and
Celeb-DF v2 datasets showed the performance of the system was
very good. The AUC was 0.985 on the FaceForensics++, and the
AUC values on all cross-dataset evaluated transfers were greater
than or equal to 0.85. The ablation studies demonstrate that the
multimodal fusion method outperforms each individual mode and
has increased robustness under the effect of compression. The
cross-dataset evaluations also showed high AUC values across all
evaluation transfers.

I. INTRODUCTION
Generative adversarial networks (GANs) and diffusion mod-
els are some examples of new generative models that have
recently been created. For example, they can be used to create
realistic synthetic video content or ‘deepfakes’. While they can
provide many advantages in entertainment and business, they
also pose serious risks for disinformation, phishing attacks,
and deteriorating trust in authority figures. Thus, detecting
deepfakes has become a significant research area within mul-
timedia forensic science. Many current techniques utilize su-
pervised visual classifiers that learn to identify manipulations
based on training data sets, such as FaceForensics++. While
most of these techniques provide high accuracy when tested
in the laboratory setting, accuracy is frequently reduced when
evaluating them in real-world conditions under resampling,
compression, or attacks that have occurred after the original
manipulation.
In our research, we improved the traditional black box
methodology used in investigating deep fakes by combining
multiple forms of evidence gathered from different sources
using a forensic reasoning process. By using different types
of indicators about validity and manipulation together, we
created a system that will allow for a reliable and thorough
review of deep fakes through the use of interpretable eval-
uations. This allows the user to understand how we came
to an evaluation and gives evidence to analysts to assist in
forensic investigations. Our approach considers the detection
of deep fakes to be more than just using a model to classify
media; instead, we approached deep fake detection evidentially
through reasoning and hence produced a system that will give
like all evidence-based reasoning models, provide a solid basis
for making reliable decisions in both high-stakes environments
and security-critical areas.
II. LITERATUREREVIEW
According to a review conducted by Rana et al. [1] that in-
cluded 112 studies (2018 to 2020) on deepfake classifications
of created deepfakes, CNN-based approaches in the realm of
deep learning provided the best performance in regards to
accuracy because of the superior learning capabilities of CNNs
to learn the hierarchical nature of high-level features and to
render subtle alterations. Challenges facing these methods of
classification include poor generalization abilities across mul-
tiple datasets and being overly sensitive to compression as well

--- Page 2 ---
as being vulnerable to advesarial attacks. In addition, Azeez et
al. [2] have conducted a review of approximately 95 studies
involving the creation and classification of deepfakes. Their
findings support the effectiveness of multimodal approaches
that rely on contrasting visual, physiological, temporal and
metadata evidence to classify deepfakes. Other findings indi-
cate the presence of biased datasets; poor performance against
previously unseen alterations of deepfakes; and they have
advocated for a modular evidence-fusion framework to help
improve robustness and scalability of deepfake classifiers.
Additionally, Selvaraju et al. [3] introduced Gradient-
Weighted Class Activation Mapping (Grad-CAM), an explain-
able AI technique that visualizes image regions influencing
CNN predictions. The proposed framework incorporates Grad-
CAM within its visual forensic module to localize manipu-
lation artifacts and provide interpretable evidence alongside
automated deepfake detection results.
Using Remote Photoplethysmography (rPPG), Ciftci et al.
[4] verified that deepfake video content can be categorized as
such based on inconsistencies between expected and actual
blood flow characteristics. Their research indicates that the
hemodynamic responses of generative models do not corre-
spond consistently; thus, rPPG can be used as an effective
modality-independent authenticity measure. This study in-
cludes rPPG among a variety of other evidence sources, which
should enhance reliability in difficult circumstances, such as
low-light conditions or media that has been recompressed.
A similar study by Li and Lyu [5] used pixel-level analysis
with convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to reveal visual
artifacts associated with creating deepfake images. However,
visual techniques generally have difficulty performing consis-
tently across datasets and with recompressed images. There-
fore, the proposed framework uses multiple modalities to
combine evidence and achieve greater reliability in detection.
The FaceForensics++ dataset was created by Rossler et al.
[6], and it is one of the largest benchmarks of manipulated
facial videos available today. The dataset includes videos sub-
jected to all types of augmentation and compression methods.
As demonstrated by Rossler et al.’s study using this dataset,
CNN classifiers operate at a high level of accuracy on this
single dataset but exhibit a poor generalization of many of
these same classifiers to other datasets. Based on this find-
ing, the proposed framework will utilize the FaceForensics++
dataset for initial training and evaluation but will also use the
DFDC and Celeb-DF v2 datasets to measure the framework’s
robustness across multiple diverse datasets.
The framework proposed by Bharati et al. [7] utilizes a
combination of CNN classifiers and human-interpretable evi-
dence to create an explainable deepfake detection framework
that aids forensic analysts in their investigations of deepfakes.
The framework proposed by Bharati et al. develops a uni-
fied cross-evidence reasoning layer, which includes metadata
analysis, physiological measurements, and visual attribution,
for analysing the agreement and disagreement within multiple
forensic findings detected across videos.
Lastly, the two visual detection backbones for the pro-
posed framework will be EfficientNet [8] (Tan and Le) and
Xception [9] (Chollet). In particular, EfficientNet-B4 balances
high accuracy with a low computational cost, while Xception
provides the ability to detect fine-level detail of manipulated
video artifacts, making it possible for detailed analysis of the
functionality of visual detection methods.

B. System Architecture
The architecture of this proposed framework is designed
to be modular rather than monolithic, with each module
processing its own type of input and producing structured
and typed outputs [11]. This architecture allows better in-
terpretability, extensibility, and fault-tolerance, as it allows

the incorporation of disparate forensic cues from multiple
different analytical domains. In contrast to monolithic systems
found in other literature which rely exclusively on a single
deep neural network [12], this proposed framework allows
for the integration of metadata forensics, physiological signal
analysis and visual artefact detection into a single framework
that allows for decision-level fusion. Each module processes a
different form of the input media and provides complementary
evidence for the final assessment of the authenticity of the
input media.
In general, the metadata forensics module focuses on deter-
mining both contradictions and anomalous data in multimedia
metadata. ExifTool and MediaInfo are examples of the tools
that extract structured metadataMfrom image and video
containers and provide details about encoding parameters,
timestamps, codecs, frame rates, GPS coordinates, compres-
sion history, and device signatures [10]. After extracting these
attributes, they can be analysed through either heuristic-based
or statistical anomaly detection methods, allowing analysts to
determine the meta data consistency scoreS m. They may look
for inconsistencies between codec signatures and device manu-
facturers, abnormal timestamp explanations, and the possibility
of several re-encodings to determine if tampering or alteration
has occurred. Because metadata will remain partially intact
after visual manipulation has been made, it is an additional
source of forensic evidence that is independent of the pixel-
related analysis.
The physiology analysis part determines if the facial behav-
iors you see from the observed facial behavior will have any
biologically plausible human behaviors. This use of the remote
photoplethysmography (rPPG) techniques (also referred to
as CHROM and POS algorithms) will estimate the subtle
variation of blood flow in the facial skin area based on the
facial structure [13]. The facial tracking will use OpenCV to
maintain a temporally stable region of interest (ROI) across all
video frames of the video. The resulting temporal representa-
tionxwill then be analyzed in the frequency domain using
Fast Fourier Transforms (FFT) and then by calculating the
signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) of the signal to help establish if the
pulse characteristics will produce a plausible result. Finally, a
physiological plausibility scoreS p is calculated based on the
coherence, periodic and stability of the recovered signal. Since
generative models of deepfake will generally be more focused
on visual reality rather than the underlying genetic basis, it is
difficult to create a video that maintains the true physiology.
The visual detection module uses deep convolutional neural
networks to analyze facial content that has been manipulated
and perform a spatial analysis. Two architectures that provide
strong results for image forensics and have proven well for
feature extraction tasks include EfficientNet and Xception [3],
[8], [9]. Like other models in this category, these architec-
tures use an input frame or input sequence to predict the
likelihood of manipulationP v while producing a set of spatial
attribution mapsLusing Gradient-Weighted Class Activation
Mapping (Grad-CAM). The spatial attribution maps allow for
the interpretability of the model’s prediction by highlighting
facial regions that had the strongest impact on the model
classification decision (eg., blended edges, inconsistency in the
texture, non-normative luminosity). This added transparency
benefits forensic investigators by aiding them in verifying the
automated predictions.
Once the meta-data, physiological and visual modules gen-
erate their respective outputs, they are then fused together
within a fusion layer via a weighted decision-level aggregation
process to produce an ultimately final output called a forensic
reportR[11]. The fusion’s approach is based on combining
the confidence levels of each of the individual modules’
outputs, along with incorporating reliability of each module
under different conditions of input data. For example, if there
are two modules generating output (i.e. meta-data, and visual)
with a meta-data gathering module generating output from a
transcoded or stripped video file, then the framework would be
able to continue using the remaining two modules generating
output; if the quality of face detection was poor, then the
framework could still conduct an analysis based on meta-
data. The use of a modular approach increases the overall

robustness, scalability, and failure resistance of the framework
when compared to using a single stream method of analysis
[7].
FFmpeg has a fixed sampling rate ofr f frames per second
for frame extraction, thus ensuring that the results produced
by frame extraction will be temporal processing results that
will be temporally comparable. Detection of facial areas in
each frame and bounding boxB i creation around detected
areas, utilized by the pretrained RetinaFace model, is accom-
plished through the use of the OpenCV library. This allows
for tracking of facial area landmarks across adjacent frames
through the use of temporal consistency (i.e., reducing noise
in the image produced due to motion and improving the
stability of the image signal) [13]. Metadata extraction is
accomplished by extracting from the raw multimedia container
header prior to decoding the frame and preserving the time-
stamping information of the original provenance, thus mini-
mizing the occurrence of potential contamination in the result
of preprocessing operations [10].
C. Module Design
1) Metadata F orensics Module:Using ExifTool v12 and
MediaInfo v23, forensic features at the encoding and container
levels are extracted. These tools provide low-level access
to multimedia container metadata, codec parameters, stream
attributes, and encoding signatures. Unlike pixel-domain anal-
ysis, metadata analysis offers insights into structural irregular-
ities introduced during content generation, editing, transcod-
ing, or recompression. Since most synthetic media undergo
multiple encoding operations, metadata inconsistencies can
serve as early indicators of manipulation even when visual
artifacts are minimal. Therefore, the proposed framework
integrates metadata analysis as an essential component of
the overall forensic pipeline, complementing both visual and
physiological analyses.
For metadata-based forensic assessment, five primary indi-
cators (K= 5) are extracted from the multimedia content.
The first indicator examines codec signatures and encoder
identifiers embedded within video containers. These include
FFmpeg tags, vendor-specific codec information, build hashes,
and software-generated metadata. Comparing these signatures
with those expected from the original source can reveal
evidence of synthetic generation or post-production modifi-
cations.
The second indicator analyzes frame-level bitrate patterns,
represented by the sequence{b i}N
i=1. Authentic videos typ-
ically exhibit smooth bitrate variations, whereas manipulated
content often introduces abnormal fluctuations. Statistical anal-
ysis of bitrate behavior is therefore used to quantify encoding
irregularities.
The third indicator evaluates the Group of Pictures (GOP)
structure, represented by{g k}. Original videos generally main-
tain a consistent keyframe insertion pattern determined by
encoder settings. Deepfake generation, editing, or re-encoding
operations may disrupt this pattern, resulting in anomalous
GOP structures that can be detected through forensic analysis.
The fourth indicator assesses temporal consistency by ex-
amining the relationship between Presentation Timestamps
(PTS) and Decoding Timestamps (DTS) throughout the video
stream. Legitimate multimedia content maintains proper tem-
poral ordering during encoding and playback. Discontinuities,
duplicated timestamps, reversals, or abnormal timing offsets
may indicate frame insertion, deletion, interpolation, or re-
construction processes introduced during manipulation.
The fifth indicator calculates the completeness of metadata
by means of a ratioρ∈[0,1]that is defined as how
many of the required fields of metadata for that multimedia
file/container, exist. This means that if a synthetic multimedia
file is transcoded or edited, there may not be any or at least
much of the standard metadata left in that file due to the
amount of processing performed on it before it was created.
Therefore, a lower ratio of completeness indicates that there is
less reliable structure in that file and the integrity of the file’s
provenance is also compromised [10].

D. Cross-Evidence Fusion and F orensic Reasoning Layer
1) Cross-Modal Consistency Modelling:Rather than ag-
gregating modality scores through simple weighted averaging,
the proposed fusion layer explicitly models theagreementand
disagreementamong heterogeneous forensic evidence streams.
This enables the system to generate interpretable forensic
hypotheses based on cross-modal consistency patterns while
preserving transparency in the decision-making process [11].

The purpose of these hypotheses is to provide interpretable
forensic explanations as opposed to supervised ground-truth
classes. Specifically, scenario 1 (H2) accounts for the existence
of metadata inconsistencies in addition to physiologically
implausible rPPG signals that jointly suggest the possibility
of the generation of synthetic content [4]. Similarly, scenario
2 (H 3) accounts for cases where physiological signals con-
tinue to be consistent with real content, even though there
are metadata anomalies indicating the possibility of post-
processing [10]. Finally, scenario 3 (H4) defines circumstances
in which there are no significant metadata anomalies but the
physiological signals are inconsistent with the original content,
which is typically viewed as evidence of facial replacement
manipulations [13].
In this study’s benchmark datasets, only two different types
of authenticity are being identified (real or manipulated);
there is no use of supervised target classes for hypotheses
H1-H4 during training either. Instead, the hypotheses exist
purely to allow the reasoning layer to provide understandable
rationale to support the final decision making from the forensic
perspective.

E. Computational Complexity and Robustness
The metadata component operates in constant time for each
video while the rPPG part scales linearly with the number of
frames (n) at the expense of a complex FFT-based spectral
analysis withO(NlogN)complexity. The visual detector is
the main computational bottleneck, requiringO(N·C cnn)
computations, which when using EfficientNet-B4 consumes
approximately 4.2 GFLOPs/frame. For example, a 10-second
video with 300 total frames will require approximately 12
seconds of inference on an(NVIDIA) A100 GPU. In order
to mitigate this issue and improve robustness against adverse
conditions, all three methods described above: i) metadata;
ii) rPPG extraction in the YCbCr colour model; and iii)
augmentation through compression/blur distortion, access the
same independent variables that allow for remarkable visual
fusion between each of the three methods. If either metadata or
a detected facial (face) of a participant has been compromised,
the three methods will apply adaptive fusion to provide reliable
inference results.
Final decisions were generated through weighted multi-
modal fusion (w 1 = 0.4,w 2 = 0.3,w 3 = 0.3) with a decision
threshold ofτ f = 0.5. Performance was evaluated using
Accuracy, F1-score, and Area Under the Receiver Operating
Characteristic Curve (AUC-ROC), with results averaged over
three independent runs.
F . Experimental Setup
The proposed framework was evaluated on three benchmark
datasets: FaceForensics++ (FF++), DeepFake Detection Chal-
lenge (DFDC), and Celeb-DF v2. FaceForensics++ served as
the primary benchmark, while DFDC and Celeb-DF v2 were
used to assess robustness and cross-dataset generalization. To
prevent data leakage, all datasets were partitioned using a
70%–15%–15% train–validation–test split with subject-level
separation.
Experiments were conducted on an NVIDIA A100 GPU.
The visual detection module employed EfficientNet-B4 and
Xception backbones initialized with ImageNet pre-trained
weights and fine-tuned using the Adam optimizer. Input facial
regions were resized to224×224pixels, and data augmen-
tation included horizontal flipping, JPEG recompression, and
Gaussian blur.
The physiological module extracted rPPG signals at 30 fps
and applied band-pass filtering within the 40–200 BPM range,
while metadata features were obtained using ExifTool and
MediaInfo. Final predictions were generated through weighted
multimodal fusion (w 1 = 0.4,w 2 = 0.3,w 3 = 0.3) with a
decision threshold ofτ f = 0.5. Performance was evaluated
using Accuracy, F1-score, and AUC-ROC, averaged across
three independent runs.

F . Cross-Modal Consistency Analysis
The framework improves reliability by jointly analyzing vi-
sual, physiological, and metadata evidence. Individual modal-
ities may provide weak manipulation cues, but their combined
agreement forms a stronger forensic narrative and increases
confidence in detection. Conversely, modality disagreements
can indicate partial manipulations or post-processing effects.
This multimodal consistency reasoning enhances robustness,
interpretability, and generalization compared with unimodal
deepfake detection approaches.
G. Discussion
The experimental results validate the central hypothesis
that multimodal forensic reasoning improves deepfake detec-
tion reliability. Across all benchmark datasets, the proposed
framework consistently outperformed unimodal configurations
while maintaining strong generalization capability under cross-
dataset evaluation. A key observation is that performance
degradation under compression remains significantly smaller
than commonly reported for purely visual detectors. This
behavior suggests that physiological and metadata-based sig-
nals provide resilience when visual artifacts become par-
tially obscured. Although promising, limitations remain. The
physiological module requires sufficient facial visibility and
video quality for reliable signal extraction. Future work will
investigate transformer-based multimodal fusion, adversarial
robustness, and large-scale real-world forensic validation.
V. CONCLUSION
The proposed framework for explainable multimodal deep-
fake forensics has demonstrated that integrating metadata
consistency analysis, physiological validation using remote
photoplethysmography (rPPG), and visual attribution has sig-
nificantly improved detection accuracy and interpretability.
Unlike many traditional unimodal methods that are focused
primarily on finding visual artifacts of manipulated videos, this
proposed framework is designed to integrate and utilize several
sources of evidence so the forensic analysis can provide a
transparent and trustworthy evaluation of authenticity. Exper-
imental results with benchmark datasets showed a substantial
improvement in performance compared to the best unimodal
baseline (an AUC of 0.985 on FaceForensics++ compared
to an AUC of 0.918 on the best unimodal baseline), with
almost all cross-dataset evaluations exceeding an AUC of 0.85;
thus, indicating that this framework generalizes well across
a variety of video manipulation techniques. The framework
also performs well in the presence of real-world degradations
(such as JPEG compression, noise, and post-processing), by
using complementary physiological and metadata signals to
provide reliable evidence when visual cues become weak-
ened. Additionally, the proposed framework can create human-
readable forensic reports, which include evidence localization,
confidence scores associated with each piece of evidence, and
interpretable explanations of the analysis performed. Finally,
an adaptive fusion approach will allow for graceful degradation
of performance in the case of partial input failure, thereby
making this framework practical, reliable, and explainable for
forensic applications in real-world scenarios.
